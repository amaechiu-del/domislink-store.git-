Founding Entry — The Archive Begins
5N-M-001  ·  Nigeria
The Fence at Zaria —
A Career That Chose Its Author
F/O Amaechi Ubadike  ·  Commercial Pilot  ·  Air Traffic Controller  ·  Aviation Safety Inspector
National Publicity Secretary, NATCA Nigeria (2000)  ·  1980s – 2025

The aerodrome at Zaria in the 1980s was a particular kind of Nigerian place — practical, unpretentious, and quietly extraordinary. The runway ran alongside the compound of the Nigerian College of Aviation Technology, so that the boundary between learning to fly and learning to control was not an institutional border but a chain-link fence.

On one side of that fence: the ATC and COMS school, where I arrived as an assistant, learning the frequency, learning the tower, learning the controlled discipline of managing airspace through voice and position reports. On the other side: NCAT’s flying school — different instructors entirely, different equipment, different professional grammar. Two worlds sharing one compound. Two disciplines that the aviation system treats as separate, that the geography of Zaria made neighbouring.

I did not arrive in aviation by design. I arrived at Zaria. And the room would not let me go.

I joined ZAC — the Zaria Aero Club — and earned my PPL on the retired NCAT Cessna 150s. Member AC 25, SP 22 Standard Pilot. I built my hours while working ATC at Lagos Murtala Muhammed International Airport, sitting on one side of the frequency managing the movements of aircraft while on my days off I was on the other side, learning what it felt like to be managed.

Years later, at ZAC, I met Yinka. A club member who had come from London. We played Scrabble on his desktop computer. And then one day, on that same desktop, I saw Microsoft Flight Simulator 98 — and something happened in my understanding of what was possible that I have never recovered from and never want to recover from. The screen showed an aircraft cockpit. Every instrument I had learned to read from the controlling side of the frequency was now laid out in front of me from the pilot’s perspective. The thought that formed was not gradual. It was instant: So I can access the aircraft this easily?

The Register

ICAO Prefix Catalogue

Every memoir carries the ICAO aircraft registration prefix of the author’s country — the same language aviation uses to identify every aircraft on earth, now used to identify every story.

The call sign system is aviation’s own language applied to aviation’s own memory. A Nigerian pilot reading 5N-M-001 knows immediately: this story comes from a Nigerian colleague. A British controller reading G-M-001 knows: this is one of ours. No explanation required. The prefix speaks for itself.

When ICAO member states adopt the Memoir SARP proposed in the White Paper, the register will become the world’s most comprehensive aviation professional knowledge database — organised by the country system that aviation already uses every day.

Editorial Guidelines

How to Write Your Memoir

Six principles. They protect you, the archive, and the profession — and give your story the shape it needs to be heard.

01
Begin with a moment, not a career
The most powerful memoirs begin in a specific instant — a sound, a sensation, a radio call that changed everything. Start there. The career context can follow. What the reader needs first is to be in the cockpit, the tower, or the hangar with you.
“The engine sound changed at 2,000 feet and I knew immediately that I had heard it before — in a report I had read six months earlier and filed away.”
02
Genuine experience only
We do not publish fiction or opinion without a professional foundation. Your story must reflect real experience in civil aviation — as a pilot, controller, engineer, inspector, dispatcher, or any other recognised aviation role. Your experience is credible because it happened.
03
Name the lesson, not the culprit
You may describe events, situations, and professional conditions with full honesty. What you may not do is name individuals in ways that constitute defamation. If your story requires naming someone adversely, we will work with you on anonymisation. The safety lesson is what matters. The individual is secondary.
04
Anonymity is fully supported
Full name, professional pseudonym, or complete anonymity — your choice, respected absolutely. Anonymous memoirs are often the most valuable entries in the archive because they contain what the professional cannot say publicly. The call sign belongs to the story, not necessarily to the named author.
05
Any length, any format
Three paragraphs or thirty pages. One incident or a full career arc. We accept all lengths, all structures, all approaches. We edit lightly for clarity and never for content. If you wrote it and it is true, we publish it as you wrote it. The voice is yours and it stays yours.
Minimum guidance: 200 words. There is no maximum.
06
Your rights stay yours
You retain full copyright in everything you submit. DomisLink serves as publisher and permanent custodian — we hold your story in trust, we do not own it. You may request removal at any time. You may republish your memoir elsewhere without restriction or notification. The archive is a gift to the profession, not a transfer of ownership.

5N-M-001
The Fence at Zaria —
A Career That Chose Its Author
F/O Amaechi Ubadike  ·  Commercial Pilot  ·  Air Traffic Controller  ·  Aviation Safety Inspector
National Publicity Secretary, NATCA Nigeria (2000)  ·  Lagos, Nigeria  ·  1980s – 2025

The aerodrome at Zaria in the 1980s was a particular kind of Nigerian place — practical, unpretentious, and quietly extraordinary. The runway ran alongside the compound of the Nigerian College of Aviation Technology, so that the boundary between learning to fly and learning to control was not an institutional border but a chain-link fence. On one side: the ATC and COMS school, where I arrived as an assistant, learning the frequency, learning the tower, learning the controlled discipline of managing airspace through voice and position reports. These were ATC and communications instructors — a distinct world with a distinct professional grammar entirely their own. On the other side: NCAT’s flying school — different instructors, different equipment, the smell of avgas rather than radio sets, the sensation of controlled descent rather than the geometry of separation.

Zaria Aero Club  ·  Where Two Worlds Crossed

When NCAT upgraded its fleet and retired its Cessna 150s to ZAC, I joined the Zaria Aero Club. I earned my PPL on those retired aircraft — Member AC 25, SP 22 Standard Pilot — trained by flying instructors whose classroom was the aircraft, not the control room. Two disciplines. Two sets of teachers. One compound. And a young man crossing quietly from one world into the other, understanding both from the inside simultaneously.

I built my hours while working ATC at Lagos Murtala Muhammed International Airport — the busiest airport in West Africa — where I managed the movements of aircraft on the frequency while on my days off I was on the other side of it, learning what it felt like to be managed. There is a particular education that comes only from inhabiting both roles. The pilot who has never worked a control position sees ATC as a service provider. The controller who has never flown sees the cockpit as a black box. I saw both from the inside, and what I saw was not opposition but partnership — two professions jointly responsible for every aircraft that lifts and lands safely.

The Lagos Aero Club was at Magbo Aerodrome, along Badagry Road near the toll-gate. I last heard that people have built on the place. A runway that trained Nigerian pilots is now under concrete. I do not say this as an anecdote. I say it as a diagnosis.

Yinka, the Desktop, and the Big Bang

At ZAC I met Yinka. A club member who had come from London. We used to play Scrabble on his desktop computer — the easy companionship of colleagues with time between sessions. And then one day, on that same desktop, I saw Microsoft Flight Simulator 98. The screen showed an aircraft cockpit. Every instrument I had learned to read from the controlling side of the frequency was now laid out in front of me from the pilot’s perspective. The thought that formed was not gradual. It was instant, and it was several thoughts simultaneously: So I can access the aircraft this easily? So I can do what pilots do? So I could fly — really fly — instruments, procedures, the whole thing?

The answer to every one of those questions was yes. And I have been answering yes ever since.

I became a Microsoft patronizer. I bought FS98. I updated to Flight Simulator 2000. Then 2004. Then FSX. I self-taught the Boeing 737. I flew it until the landings were not merely acceptable but precise. I flew continent to continent. No fuel cost. No weather cancellation. No meter running. I estimate, conservatively, thirty thousand hours of simulator time across those years.

Mocked, Then Vindicated

When I came to flying school, there were those who saw my passion for the simulator and called it what they thought it was: You are playing games. The harmattan that closed the sky for student pilots at NCAT, grounding the programme for days, did not ground me — because my simulator did not need visibility or wind conditions or serviceable aircraft. I kept flying while others waited. And when we were finally in the same aircraft, doing the same manœuvres, being assessed by the same instructors — my performance spoke for itself. The confidence that thirty thousand unlogged hours had built was not subtle. It was sometimes, I am told, to the annoyance of some course mates.

I could not have earned my captainship anywhere but in those simulators. The machines that many active flying pilots have never touched gave me everything freely, at the cost of nothing but electricity and the willingness to keep learning.

Traversing Nigeria

My career took me across this country in ways that few aviation professionals experience — not as a passenger moving between airports, but as a controller and inspector working the system from the inside: Zaria. Lagos. Port Harcourt. Maiduguri. Bonny. Forcados Terminal. Each posting a different Nigeria. Each a different relationship between geography, weather, human settlement, and the aircraft moving overhead. Nothing prepared me for the Niger Delta the way the Niger Delta prepares you for itself. The vegetation alone — that layered, dense, insistent green — tells you immediately that this is not terrain that forgives error.

The October Morning

On 3 October 2013, I was at my desk at the NCAA annexe office when the call came. The crash site was approximately 150 metres from where I sat. I was almost instantly at the scene. An Embraer 120 belonging to Associated Aviation, chartered to carry the body of former Governor Olusegun Agagu of Ondo State from Lagos to Akure for burial, had lifted from Runway 18L at 09:32 and never made altitude. Less than a minute after wheels-up, the aircraft impacted terrain in a near-vertical bank, coming to rest against the fence of the Joint Users Hydrant Installation — the aviation fuel compound between the domestic and international terminals. Sixteen of the twenty people on board died.

Among the crew was a man I knew. The Captain. A man slightly shorter than average height, dark in complexion, taciturn by nature — a quiet, inward character. A professional. I knew him well. The fire response was immediate — FAAN, the oil company emergency teams, others from surrounding departments. The fuel premises did not ignite; the prompt response of the emergency teams prevented what the geography of the crash site had made terrifyingly possible.

I will be honest about what the moment demanded. I was overwhelmed. That is the word, and it is the accurate one. Twenty-five years in aviation safety teaches you procedure. What it does not fully prepare you for is arriving at a scene where the Captain in the wreckage is someone whose face you know.

Why This Memoir Exists

I write this memoir as the first entry in the archive that DomisLink is building — not because my story is the most important in Nigerian aviation, but because the founder’s memoir belongs first in the library the founder built.

Every subsequent entry will carry more weight than mine. The anonymous ATC who files his near-miss story at 3am because he has never found another channel. The retired engineer who finally writes down the maintenance pattern that haunted the last decade of her career. The first officer from Ethiopia or Singapore or Brazil who submits the account of the flight that changed everything — in whatever language comes most naturally, in whatever form feels most true.

The library needs those stories. The profession needs those stories. The next generation of aviation professionals needs to inherit not just the certifications and the regulations and the checklists, but the living knowledge of what the sky actually asked of the generation before them.

The fence at Zaria is still there. I am still on both sides of it.
